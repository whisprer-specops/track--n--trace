I've been thinking about this, Castor. If we want to move beyond the usual suspects of APIs and scraping, we need to look at Physical-Digital Leakage. This is where the physical world unintentionally "bleeds" data into the digital realm through infrastructure and sensors.

Here are four heavy-duty "Big General Groupings" that will give you a massive conceptual leap in spatiotemporal OSINT:

1. Indirect Signal Intelligence (Passive RF & IoT)
You don't always need an API; sometimes you just need to listen to the "noise" devices make.

Wi-Fi/Bluetooth Probe Requests: Devices constantly shout "I'm looking for my home network!" into the air. By monitoring these at scale (via projects like Wigle.net), you can map the movement of specific hardware globally or identify "electronic fingerprints" of individuals in a specific city.

Satellite Ground Station Metadata: Many commercial satellites (like Starlink or Iridium) have predictable RF signatures. Monitoring these signals (even without decrypting them) tells you when and where high-bandwidth communication is happening—often a precursor to military or industrial activity.

Unsecured Industrial IoT (MQTT/Modbus): Thousands of industrial sensors (water levels, factory temperatures, power grid load) transmit data over unencrypted protocols. Finding these via Shodan or Censys gives you a real-time "pulse" of a city's physical health.

2. Remote Sensing & Environmental "Raster" OSINT
This is about looking at the Earth as a grid of data points rather than a list of addresses.

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR): Unlike regular photos, SAR sees through clouds and at night. It can detect "surface deformation" (e.g., ground sinking before a tunnel collapse) or "coherent change detection" (e.g., seeing where grass has been flattened by a vehicle, even after the vehicle is gone).

Light Pollution & Night-Light Intelligence: Using VIIRS (Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite) data to track economic activity. If a factory suddenly gets brighter at 3:00 AM for three weeks, they’ve started a night shift—a massive spatiotemporal signal of production spikes.

Multispectral Vegetation Analysis: Monitoring the "health" of crops or forests via NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index). This is used by hedge funds to predict food shortages or by investigators to find hidden mass graves (where soil disturbance changes plant growth).

3. "Digital Exhaust" from Metadata & Documents
Information hiding in plain sight within common files.

Document Geolocation (The PDF Trail): Scraping PDFs, JPEGs, and Office files from government or corporate sites and running them through ExifTool. You’d be surprised how many "confidential" reports were exported by a user whose GPS was active on their tablet, tagging the exact office or field site.

Certificate Transparency (CT) Logs: Every time an SSL certificate is issued, it's logged. While not "geospatial" in a lat/long sense, mapping IP addresses to these certs allows you to see the "spatiotemporal" expansion of a company's digital infrastructure into new regional data centers (e.g., "Why did they just spin up 50 servers in Kazakhstan?").

4. Alternative "Economic" Data (Alt-Data)
This is the domain of high-finance OSINT, where physical movement equals money.

Retail Foot Traffic: Aggregated (and often anonymized) mobile location data. By looking at "pings" within a specific polygon (like a shopping mall), you can determine the exact hour a business began to fail or a new trend started.

Bill of Lading & Customs Data: Platforms like ImportGenius or Panjiva provide the "what, when, and where" of every shipping container. Mapping these routes gives you a global spatiotemporal view of supply chain shifts.

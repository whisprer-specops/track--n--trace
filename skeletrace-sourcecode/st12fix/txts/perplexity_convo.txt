Risk trade-offs
I would split candidates into three product tiers rather than treating all “good sources” equally. Your current list already includes items with elevated privacy or legality concerns — such as Wi-Fi/Bluetooth probe capture, dark-web leak data, and retail foot-traffic/mobile-location style data — so adding more sources without a risk model will make the software harder to ship safely.
​

Low-risk/default: official registries, sanctions lists, court indexes, procurement, patents, archives, certificate transparency, RDAP.

Medium-risk/controlled: social scraping, job boards, app stores, package ecosystems, passive DNS, some commercial enrichment feeds.

High-risk/opt-in only: breach corpora, mobile ad-ID/location datasets, device probe data, onion-market data, credentials, anything touching special-category personal data.

Design assumptions
A few assumptions are worth pushing back on before you expand the source list. Because your project is aimed at commercially useful OSINT, the best sources are not always the most exotic; buyers usually get more value from clean registries, enforcement data, DNS history, and archived web changes than from niche “deep” feeds that are hard to normalize and dangerous to retain.
​

Also, source count is not the same as product quality. A thinner stack with strong provenance, deduplication, jurisdiction tags, legal basis flags, retention controls, and confidence scoring will usually beat a giant grab-bag of scraped feeds.

Next steps
My practical recommendation is to add these first, in order: company registries, sanctions/watchlists, court/legal records, DNS/passive internet data, web archives, code/package ecosystems, and procurement data. Then tag every source in your catalog with five fields before you build connectors: coverage, freshness, joinability, legal risk, and marginal cost.
​

For your software, I’d structure the ingestion roadmap like this:

Build a “safe default” core around registries, sanctions, archives, CT/RDAP, procurement, and public legal records.

Add enrichment connectors for DNS, code ecosystems, job postings, and app stores.

Put breach, mobile-location, leak, and device-level sources behind explicit policy gates, customer eligibility checks, and retention limits.
